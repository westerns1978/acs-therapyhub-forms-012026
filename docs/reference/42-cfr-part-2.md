# 42 CFR Part 2 — Plain-Language Reference

> **Educational reference only.** This explains the federal confidentiality rules for substance use records in plain terms. It is **not** legal advice, and it does **not** describe any specific client's consent status — that lives in each client's record.

Because ACS provides substance use disorder treatment, its records are protected by **both HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2** — and Part 2 is stricter than HIPAA. Understanding the difference matters most for court-involved, probation-referred, and attorney-represented clients.

---

## The core idea

HIPAA generally lets covered entities share protected health information for **Treatment, Payment, and Healthcare Operations (TPO)** without separate patient consent. **Part 2 does not** — for these substance-use records, written patient consent is generally required even for TPO disclosures.

## What the 2024 update changed

Under the February 2024 final rule, a patient *may* now sign a **single comprehensive written consent** authorizing future TPO uses and disclosures, which reduces repetitive paperwork and helps care coordination.

But there's a hard line that did **not** relax:

## The rule that matters most: legal consent is separate

**Consent for use in legal proceedings cannot be combined with TPO consent.** Any disclosure of records for civil, criminal, administrative, or legislative proceedings against the patient must be authorized on a **separate, standalone consent form.** Combining the two is prohibited.

This is why a clean separation between general-care consent and legal-disclosure consent isn't bureaucratic nicety — it's a federal requirement, and it bears directly on ACS's court-referred clients.

## The redisclosure notice (§ 2.32)

Every disclosure made under patient consent must carry the **§ 2.32 notice**, which tells the receiving party they are **prohibited from re-disclosing** the records further unless the consent or Part 2 expressly permits it. Once a HIPAA-covered recipient gets a Part 2 record under TPO consent, standard HIPAA redisclosure rules apply — *except* it still cannot be used against the patient in legal proceedings.

---

## What this means day-to-day

- Keep general-care (TPO) consent and legal-proceeding consent on **separate forms.**
- Every consented disclosure should carry the **§ 2.32 redisclosure notice.**
- Consents have lifecycles — active, expired, revoked — and disclosures should be logged.
- When in doubt about a legal or court disclosure, treat it as the higher-sensitivity path and confirm a valid standalone legal consent exists first.
